The challenge

Full hospital data standardisation requires three components: LOINC (what you measure), SNOMED (how you measure it), and UCUM (the unit). Without SNOMED, standardisation is incomplete.

SNOMED is a multi hierarchical ontology, every clinical concept has parents, children, siblings, and attributes. When two hospitals compare lab values, SNOMED ensures you know why values differ, even if they're not identical.

The primary user is a clinical chemist, someone who likely standardises LOINC codes in the same workflow.

"How do you turn something this complex into something a doctor can grasp at 4pm on a friday?"

The underlying SNOMED decision tree, mapping a hospital term can branch into exact matches, attribute refinements, or multiple separate concepts

Understanding the decision tree

Together with Leon (clinical expert), I mapped out the full decision logic a clinical chemist goes through when mapping a hospital term to SNOMED.

The core branching logic:

  • Exact match? → done. ~80% of parameters.
  • Not specific enough? → refine or add an attribute (Step 2)
  • Multiple separate concepts? → add an extra term (Step 3)

This decision tree became the structural backbone of the interface: three steps, in order.

Three steps, in the right order

1

Choose a term

The most critical step, handling ~80% of all parameters. AI suggestions appear immediately, with a search bar for manual lookup of the full SNOMED database. Once a term is selected, parents and children are shown so the user can navigate the hierarchy and confirm the right level of specificity.

2

Adjust attributes

For the remaining cases where the standard term isn't specific enough: attributes provide extra context that refines the term precisely. This step maps directly to the "refine attribute" branch of the decision tree. It's an advanced step and requires SNOMED knowledge.

3

Add an extra term

Some parameters consist of multiple separate concepts. Step 3 maps to the "multiple SNOMED concepts" branch of the decision tree, letting the user attach additional terms to a single hospital parameter without losing accuracy.

A working prototype, not a static mockup

Without real search results and basic SNOMED knowledge, the screen is untestable. A static prototype wouldn't cut it.

So I built a fully working demo using Claude Code, connected to a Typesense backend containing the complete SNOMED database. You can search any SNOMED term, navigate the full hierarchy, and simulate a realistic standardisation workflow.

This makes the upcoming user test with clinical chemists and microbiologists more meaningful than any clickthrough prototype could be.

Key design decisions

Built on the LOINC pattern. The SNOMED screen inherits the layout structure from the LOINC standardisation screen. The same table on top, the same panel on the right. LOINC had already been tested with users and validated. Repeating those patterns meant clinical chemists could recognise the context immediately, even when the content is new.

Numbers as wayfinding. The three-step numbering directly mirrors the decision tree. A user who doesn't need step 2 or 3 can ignore them. We expect that ~80% of the data can be standardized with step 1 alone.

AI suggestions reduce cognitive load at the hardest moment. Step 1 choosing the right term is where the most critical errors happen. AI suggestions surface the most likely match immediately, so the clinical chemist validates rather than searches. The hierarchy view (parents, children, siblings) then lets them confirm or navigate to something more or less specific.

~80% of cases, one step. The design is explicitly optimised for the majority case: find the right term in step 1, click Standardize, move on. The interface doesn't ask doctors to think about attributes or multi-term expressions until they actually need to.

Status and reflection

The design is currently at about 60%. The concept is complete and the working demo is ready for testing. The first user test with known clinical chemists or microbiologists is the next step

Reflection: The biggest design challenge wasn't the interface, it was resisting the urge to present the end user with the full complexity of SNOMED. Every time I could simplify a decision, the screen got better. The interface should feel like three clear questions.
